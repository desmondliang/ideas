Today I spent three hours listening to Julie talk about her personal problems.

Three hours.

And halfway through it, a voice in my head kept asking: *What does this actually have to do with me?*

I don’t mean that in a cruel way. But as I get older — as a father, a husband, a business owner, a friend — I’m realizing that my attention is no longer an unlimited resource. It’s finite. Scarce. And I’ve been spending it like I have an infinite supply.

Lately my thoughts feel fuzzy. Like static. I’m being pulled in so many directions that it’s hard to hold onto one clear line of thinking. And instead of protecting my focus, I sabotage it. I turn on background noise. Podcasts. YouTube. Something playing in the distance.

I tell myself it helps.

If something is always moving, always talking, always happening, it creates the illusion that I’m being productive. That I’m “in motion.” But it’s a lie. It numbs my nerves and fractures my concentration. It’s noise disguised as momentum.

This week my wife and two sons are away, so it’s just me and my little girl at home.

Our rhythm looks simple on paper:

- She’s awake for six hours.
- She naps for two or three.
- I get a couple of hours after she sleeps.

In theory, that’s five focused work hours a day. That’s not nothing. A lot can be done in five sharp hours.

But they’re not sharp.

They’re interrupted. Fragmented. Diluted.

And on top of that, I keep making myself too available.

Sarah texted me today: she found a spider in the shop.

Why does that need to involve me?

Then my dad called me about a news report he saw on a Hong Kong TV channel. It was talking about people in Japan fighting over toilet paper because of a shortage.

Fake news.

But now I’m explaining supply chains and panic buying and media exaggeration instead of doing my work.

And it’s not that any one of these things is catastrophic. It’s that every single day, it’s a dozen tiny things. A spider. A rumor. A three-hour emotional download. A background podcast I didn’t need.

Tiny things that chip away at focus.

The bigger problem isn’t that people bring these things to me. The bigger problem is that I feel compelled to respond. Because if I don’t, I’m afraid I’ll lose credibility. I’m afraid silence will look like negligence.

But I’m starting to see the opposite might be true.

If I react to everything, I train people to bring me everything.

If I respond to every minor issue, I create a future where I’m the emotional and operational dumpster for everyone’s small anxieties.

And that’s on me.

Being a man in multiple roles means you cannot fight every battle. You cannot carry every conversation. You cannot be the sounding board for every fear, every spider, every three-hour spiral, every fake news headline.

You have to choose.

You have to be intentional about:

- Which conversations deserve your time.
- Which problems are actually yours.
- Which distractions are self-inflicted.
- Which silences are necessary.

Focus isn’t about time management.

It’s about courage.

The courage to not react.  
The courage to not explain.  
The courage to let small things stay small.

Because the real work — the meaningful work — requires clarity. And clarity requires subtraction.

Not more input.  
Not more noise.  
Not more availability.

Less.

I need to learn how to eliminate these distractions — not by controlling other people, but by controlling my response.

That might be the real work.

That’s the lesson for today.
